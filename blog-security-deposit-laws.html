Security deposit laws by state: what mid-sized landlords need to track

Compliance·7 min read·Updated April 2026

At five units, you can probably keep security deposit rules in your head. At fifty, across move-ins and move-outs happening every month, that stops being realistic — and the cost of getting it wrong goes up with every unit you add.

Why this gets harder as you scale, not easier

Security deposit law in the US is set state by state, and in some places, city by city. The rules that typically vary include how much you're allowed to collect as a deposit, how it needs to be held (some states require a separate escrow-style account, sometimes with interest paid to the tenant), how long you have to return it after move-out, and exactly what an itemized deduction list needs to include.

A landlord with a handful of units in one building can track this informally. A management company running 80 units — even in a single state — is processing move-outs constantly, and a missed deadline or an incomplete itemization on even one of them can turn a routine deposit return into a dispute or a small-claims filing.

The categories worth tracking systematically

  • Maximum deposit amount. Often expressed as a multiple of monthly rent, and sometimes different for furnished versus unfurnished units.
  • Holding requirements. Whether the deposit must sit in a separate account, and whether interest accrues to the tenant.
  • Return deadline. The number of days you have after move-out to return the deposit or provide an itemized list of deductions — this is one of the most commonly enforced deadlines, and penalties for missing it are often disproportionate to the amount at stake.
  • Itemization requirements. What counts as acceptable documentation for a deduction — photos, receipts, and a clear description of normal wear and tear versus damage.
  • Move-in condition documentation. Some states require a documented move-in inspection for a landlord to later withhold for damage at all.

"Normal wear and tear" is where most disputes start

Almost every state distinguishes between damage a tenant is responsible for and normal wear and tear, which a landlord is expected to absorb as a cost of doing business. Worn carpet after a multi-year tenancy is typically wear and tear; a large stain from a specific incident typically isn't. Because this line is judgment-based rather than a fixed rule, the strongest protection is documentation — dated move-in and move-out photos for every unit, not just the ones that end up in dispute.

This is general information, not legal advice

Deposit limits, timelines, and documentation requirements shift by state and change over time, and city-level rules can add another layer on top. Nothing here should be treated as a substitute for checking your specific state and local requirements, ideally with a landlord-tenant attorney or your state's housing authority, before setting deposit policy across a portfolio.

Building the habit that scales

The practical fix isn't memorizing every state's rule — it's building a process that applies the right rule automatically at every move-in and move-out: the correct maximum deposit, the correct holding requirement, and a return deadline that's tracked the same way it's tracked for every other unit in the portfolio. That consistency is what turns compliance from a risk you're managing manually into something the system handles for you.
